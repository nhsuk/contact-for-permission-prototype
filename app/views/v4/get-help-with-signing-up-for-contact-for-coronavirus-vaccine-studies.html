Get answers to common questions about coronavirus vaccine studies and signing up to be contacted about taking part

How can I change my details once I've signed up?

There is not currently a way to change your details. If you need to make a change, for example if you've had a positive coronavirus test, you need to withdraw your permission and sign up using the service again.

How can I withdraw my permission if I no longer want to be contacted?

You can use this service to withdraw your permission at any time. The link to withdraw your permission is also in the confirmation email you were sent when you signed up to be contacted for coronavirus research.

Who has access to my details if I sign up to be contacted for coronavirus research?

Only researchers working on studies approved to take place in the NHS will be able to see your details when you sign up. They'll access this information directly through NHS Digital, which holds the information securely.

Do I have to take part in a study if I sign up to be contacted?

No. By signing up to be contacted you allow researchers to contact you about specific studies. They'll give you more information about the study, and you'll be able to ask questions. You can then decide if you want to take part or not. It's your choice.

Will I be asked to join a study that's far away from where I live?

Coronavirus vaccine research is taking place across the UK so it's likely there'll be some research taking place near you. If a researcher asks you to take part in a study, you can say no if you feel it's too far away.

I'm a researcher, can I access the information of people who have signed up?

Only researchers working on specific coronavirus vaccine studies can access this information. If you have any queries about this, please use the contact form on the Be Part of Research website.

Could the vaccine give me coronavirus?

No. The vaccines are designed so they do not cause the infection.

What are the side effects I might get if I join a vaccine study?

Common vaccine side effects include soreness, swelling and redness at the site of the vaccination. You may also get more general symptoms like tiredness, aching muscles and a high temperature. These may last for a few days and usually get better by themselves.

How are studies approved?

An overall plan for the research study is called a protocol. Before a study can start, the protocol needs to be approved by a group of researchers who are not involved in the study. This approval is called an independent scientific review, or peer review.

An independent research ethics committee will also review the protocol. This committee is responsible for looking after your rights, safety, dignity and wellbeing when you take part in research. It can also decide whether the study can go ahead.

Researchers are not allowed to change the protocol without telling the ethics committee. The ethics committee can stop a study at any time if it has any concerns about the welfare of people taking part.
